================================================================================
TASK OVERVIEW
================================================================================

This analysis addresses two core questions about restaurant cuisine data:

  1. What are the most common combinations of cuisines served by restaurants?
  2. Do certain cuisine combinations tend to receive higher ratings from customers?

The goal is to extract actionable insights from cuisine labeling patterns in a
large restaurant dataset, enabling data-driven understanding of how cuisine
variety and pairing influence customer satisfaction.

================================================================================
UNDERSTANDING THE DATA
================================================================================

Each restaurant in the dataset is tagged with one or more cuisine types in a
single comma-separated field (e.g., "North Indian, Chinese, Mughlai"). This
multi-label structure allows us to:

  - Identify individual cuisine popularity
  - Extract all pairwise and triplet combinations
  - Cross-reference combinations with aggregate customer ratings

Key fields used in this analysis:

  ┌──────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Field                │ Role                                         │
  ├──────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Cuisines             │ Source of combination extraction             │
  │ Aggregate rating     │ Customer satisfaction metric (0.0 – 5.0)     │
  │ Restaurant Name      │ Identification                               │
  │ City                 │ Geographic reference                         │
  │ Votes                │ Engagement metric (not used directly)        │
  └──────────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────────┘

================================================================================
METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

STEP 1 — DATA CLEANING
  - Removed rows where the Cuisines field was missing (NaN)
  - Filtered out restaurants with an Aggregate rating of 0.0, as zero
    indicates an unrated entry rather than a genuinely low-rated one
  - Final clean dataset: 7,394 records used for rating-based analysis

STEP 2 — CUISINE PARSING
  - Split each Cuisines string by comma delimiter
  - Stripped leading/trailing whitespace from each cuisine label
  - Stored parsed results as Python lists for combination analysis

STEP 3 — FREQUENCY ANALYSIS
  - Counted occurrences of each individual cuisine (using Counter)
  - Extracted all ordered pairs (2-combinations) from each restaurant's list
  - Extracted all ordered triplets (3-combinations) from each restaurant's list
  - Used itertools.combinations() to ensure each pair/triplet is counted once
    regardless of the order in which cuisines appeared in the source field

STEP 4 — RATING ANALYSIS
  - Grouped restaurants by cuisine pair and computed the mean Aggregate rating
  - Applied a minimum frequency threshold (≥ 5 restaurants) to ensure that
    the average rating of a pair is statistically meaningful and not skewed
    by a single outlier restaurant
  - Compared mean ratings across restaurants grouped by number of cuisines
    (Single / 2 / 3 / 4+)

STEP 5 — CO-OCCURRENCE MATRIX
  - Built a symmetric matrix for the top 10 cuisines
  - Each cell (i, j) represents how many restaurants serve both cuisine i
    and cuisine j simultaneously
  - Visualized as a heatmap for intuitive pattern recognition

================================================================================
KEY FINDINGS & INTERPRETATION
================================================================================

FINDING 1 — DOMINANT PAIR: Chinese + North Indian (1,479 restaurants)
  This combination is by a significant margin the most common pairing in the
  dataset. It reflects a market preference in South Asian urban dining markets
  where both cuisines are individually popular, and their combination serves
  a broad customer base efficiently.

FINDING 2 — NORTH INDIAN IS A UNIVERSAL ANCHOR
  North Indian cuisine appears in 7 of the top 10 most common pairs. This
  indicates it functions as a "base cuisine" — restaurants add it alongside
  other offerings to maximize audience reach.

FINDING 3 — MOST COMMON TRIPLET: Chinese + Mughlai + North Indian (314)
  This triplet builds directly on the dominant pair, incorporating Mughlai
  (a North Indian subtype) as a natural extension, creating a comprehensive
  Indian dining experience with Chinese variety.

FINDING 4 — HIGHLY-RATED PAIRS ARE NICHE AND SPECIALIZED
  The top-rated cuisine pairs (e.g., Asian + Indian: 4.54 / 5.0,
  Sandwich + Seafood: 4.43 / 5.0) are not the most common ones. They appear
  in relatively few restaurants but consistently achieve high customer
  satisfaction. This suggests a quality-over-quantity dynamic — specialized
  or internationally-blended menus tend to impress more than volume-based
  multi-cuisine offerings.

FINDING 5 — MORE CUISINES = SLIGHTLY HIGHER RATINGS
  Restaurants offering 4 or more cuisine types average a rating of 3.61,
  compared to 3.40 for single-cuisine restaurants. While the difference is
  modest, it is consistent. This may reflect that multi-cuisine restaurants:
    (a) Target diverse customer groups, generating more votes
    (b) Signal a higher level of culinary ambition or investment
    (c) Attract customers with varied preferences, improving satisfaction

FINDING 6 — HIGHEST-RATED INDIVIDUAL CUISINE: Brazilian (4.34 / 5.0)
  Brazilian restaurants, while fewer in number (23 in the cleaned dataset),
  consistently score among the highest. Niche international cuisines appear
  to outperform mainstream options, possibly due to the novelty effect and
  the higher price point typically associated with them.

================================================================================
CONCLUSION
================================================================================

This analysis reveals two distinct dimensions of cuisine combination behavior:

  VOLUME DIMENSION:
    The most common combinations — dominated by North Indian, Chinese, and
    Mughlai — reflect market saturation in mainstream South Asian restaurant
    markets. High frequency does not correlate with high rating.

  QUALITY DIMENSION:
    The highest-rated combinations tend to be less common, more specialized,
    and often internationally-inspired (Asian + Indian, European + Mediterranean,
    Brazilian). Niche, well-executed combinations outperform volume-driven ones.

  PRACTICAL IMPLICATION:
    For a restaurant seeking to maximize customer ratings, this analysis suggests
    that specializing in a distinctive or internationally-blended cuisine pairing,
    rather than following the majority trend, may lead to better customer
    satisfaction outcomes.
